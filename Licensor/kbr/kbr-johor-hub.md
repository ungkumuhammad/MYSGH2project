2 H2ACT® Ammonia Cracking Technology
The market for ammonia dissociation for producing low carbon hydrogen is new, however
the technology elements to dissociate ammonia at scale are established and proven in
operation, bearing great resemblance to steam methane reforming of natural gas for syngas
generation.
KBR is ready to license and guarantee H2ACT® technology today because it is based on most
proven, reliable technology elements and process operations, available in the market today,
built on KBR legacy of success and records in the ammonia industry.
2.1 General Technology Overview
The following is the simplified block flow diagram for a typical H2ACT® ammonia cracking
plant.
Figure 2.1 - Ammonia Cracking Block-flow Diagram
In the KBR flow-scheme, ammonia dissociation is driven by heat from a fuel-fired cracker.
The ammonia cracking furnace design is based on the well-proven KBR down-fired primary
reformer design with hundreds of references from small to large scale capacity and decades
of experience and know-how.
The cracking reaction is performed under presence of nickel-based catalyst with heat
supplied by combustion of clean fuel via down-firing burners. The clean heat source for the
cracker is fuel from the process, consisting of tail-gas from the hydrogen purification section,
supplemented by cracked gas (H2/N2). Optionally the fuel for the cracker can include natural
gas.
The ammonia cracking furnace is designed to attain maximum thermal efficiency by utilizing
process waste gases as part of the fuel, as well as by recovering heat in the convection section
from the flue gases for feed vaporization, preheat and dissociation.
The furnace convection section is designed with a selective catalytic reduction (SCR) system
as standard (with optional two SCR units in-series), able to meet the most stringent
environmental emission requirements.

▪ Catalyst: Partner with world-leading catalyst suppliers for catalyst evaluation and
benchmarking. Consider both conventional proven catalysts and novel catalysts,
selecting most suitable catalyst for each client and application based on performance
and commercial readiness.
▪ Reactor: Leverage strengths in furnace design from KBR ammonia reformer and olefin

successful operation, adapted and optimized for application to ammonia dissociation.

and operating costs. Flow scheme is optimised and tailored to individual client design
basis requirements for best levelized cost of production for each application.
As with any new technology, cracking ammonia is expected to advance in the short-to-
medium term. KBR anticipates a flexible and pro-active approach to design and engineering
of the first commercial units, ensuring that technology advances (such as catalysts or burner

result, KBR’s low risk, maximum opportunity flow-scheme can support first-mover advantage
in this new market, leveraging on experience of successful technology commercialization
over 80 years.

The first area of development is the use of a well-proven equipment design (reforming

conditions, fuel mix and mechanical integrity design considerations are updated as compared
to a steam methane reforming furnace.

3.2 Block flow diagram and Process description
The following is the block flow diagram for a typical ammonia cracking facility, including all
process unit operations for delivery of high pressure, high purity hydrogen.
Figure 7 – Typical H2ACT® Block Flow Diagram
The KBR H2ACT® flow-scheme is based on thermal cracking, with ammonia dissociation
driven by heat from a fuel-fired cracker.
Liquid ammonia from storage is pumped to the required system pressure. The liquid
ammonia feedstock is preheated and vaporized via heat integration within the process.
Liquid ammonia vaporization is also partially provided by excess LP steam available from the
small steam system of the ammonia cracking plant.
Vaporized ammonia is preheated via heat integration in the process and subsequently by
recovering heat from the convection section of the fuel fired ammonia cracker, before being
directed to the reactor system for dissociation.
The reactor system consists of an adiabatic reactor (pre-cracker) in series with an isothermal,
fuel-fired cracker (ammonia cracking furnace or ammonia cracker). The cracking reaction is
performed under presence of nickel-based catalyst in both reactors.
All ammonia dissociation (for both reactors) is driven by heat from the fuel-fired ammonia
cracker. The ammonia cracking furnace design is based on the well-proven KBR down-fired
primary reformer design with hundreds of references in ammonia production, from small to
large scale capacity and decades of experience and knowhow.

The heat source for the adiabatic reactor is high-grade heat from the convection section of
the ammonia cracking furnace.
The heat source for the ammonia cracking furnace is cracked gas (H /N ) along with a very
2 2
small off-gas stream from the ammonia recovery unit (or optionally supplemented by
external fuel such as natural gas).
The ammonia cracking furnace is designed to attain maximum thermal efficiency by utilizing
process waste gases as part of the fuel, as well as by recovering heat in the convection section
from the flue gases for feed vaporization, preheat and dissociation.
The furnace convection section is furnished with a Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) system
as standard, able to meet the most stringent environmental emission requirements.
The cracked product purification is based on well-proven and often-used processes,
Ammonia Recovery for ammonia removal and PSA for nitrogen removal (optional).
The Ammonia Recovery unit removes ammonia by simple water-based ammonia absorption
/ distillation – designed and in operation is a multitude of KBR ammonia plants worldwide.
After this unit, the hydrogen purity is 75 mol% dry.
This cracked gas can be used as the final product in some projects. For most of other projects,
a vendor package PSA unit is specified in the flow-scheme to remove nitrogen and purify
hydrogen. The PSA unit is capable of delivering hydrogen purity to 99.97 mol% or higher.
PSA tail gas is sent to fuel system as a clean fuel for the fired cracker.
Typical hydrogen delivery pressure of around 30 barg is achievable without additional
hydrogen compression. Higher pressure hydrogen if required will be delivered using a small
hydrogen compressor. For Gentari cracking plant, no hydrogen compression is required to
deliver Hydrogen product at the battery limit pressure of 20 barg.
H2ACT® cracking process is designed to have self-sufficient steam balance as standard,
meaning that all steam is generated and consumed internally with no OSBL steam import or
export.

2. 50% NG Fuel mode: combination of PSA Tail Gas with fuel top-up split in 50:50

3.4 Catalysts & Chemicals
As of today, there are dozens of small ammonia cracking units operated using conventional
commercialized Nickel catalyst provided by major catalyst vendors (e.g. Clariant, etc.).
Therefore, catalyst for ammonia cracking is already available.
Recently, many developments on improving the Nickel catalyst (Ni enhanced) have been
progressed in order to increase its activ5ity at lower operating temperatures and higher
operating pressures, which are the favorable and efficient operating conditions for large
scale ammonia cracking plants.
In parallel, a number of catalyst suppliers also look at precious metal catalysts (e.g.
ruthenium). For ruthenium catalyst higher activity is predicted, at lower operating
temperature for the same conversion as a Ni-based catalyst, which can increase the efficiency
of the ammonia cracker. This has been illustrated at low pressures with ongoing research
into higher pressure operation. The downside of Ru catalyst is technology readiness and its
scarcity, leading to high cost and extreme volatility of market. Additional research, testing
and data is required to confirm benefits and economic viability of Ruthenium based catalyst
use in large scale, commercial ammonia cracking applications.
KBR generally proposes Nickel-based catalysts due to the quantity required for large scale
applications, technology readiness, availability, and cost-effectiveness. The following

3.7 Flexibility and Turndown
The KBR ammonia cracking unit can operate at turndown rate of 40% with minimal loss in
performance and efficiency. The plant can be designed for lower turndown if required with
some efficiency loss and lower hydrogen recovery from the PSA purification unit. Equipment
will be designed with design margins to enable satisfactory operation at this low turndown
rate. Product quality can be maintained throughout the operating envelope of the plant. In
addition, it is possible to design H2ACT® unit with a hot-standby capability (i.e. weekend hot-
standby with zero hydrogen production and plant in operation with hydrogen production
during weekdays) with some modifications to the flow-scheme, bearing some CAPEX/OPEX
compromise.
The ramp-up / ramp-down rate for KBR cracking plants is 0.5% per minute as standard. This
rate of change is proven to be safe and reliable in operation of KBR furnaces and control
systems whilst providing adequate operational flexibility. This can be further improved if
there are project specific drivers for a higher load change rate, bearing additional CAPEX
and/or OPEX and coupled with hydrogen storage.
3.8 Plant Availability
In KBR experience, the typical recent benchmark for turnaround period for similar industrial
plants with reforming furnaces (SMRs) is 4 years with 30-40 days for maintenance works.
As compared to a traditional hydrogen production facility, the ammonia cracking plant has
fewer equipment, and the number of rotary equipment is much lower. The main ammonia
feed is pumped instead of compressed which increases the reliability of feed supply as one
stand-by pump will be available to start in automatic. The hydrogen compressor is also fully
spared with a similar arrangement. Therefore, on-stream time for the ammonia cracking unit
of 8,400 hours per calendar year is realistic and achievable for KBR equipment design and
technology.
Generally, time required between turnaround is dependent on turnaround period for
rotating equipment and the catalyst life. To-date, some catalyst suppliers for ammonia
dissociation catalysts are able to guarantee a 2 year life (this is expected to improve to 4
years over time with additional testing and operational experience). Based on experience of
steam reforming catalyst loading / unloading schedules, this activity can take 12-15 days. As
a result, a turnaround period of 2 years with 20 days duration can be envisaged for the first
years of operation of the ammonia cracking unit, maintaining an overall onstream factor of
8,400 hours per calendar year.
In time, KBR expect that the period between turnarounds and inspections can be increased
to 4 years with longer TAR duration, maintaining an on-stream factor of 8,400 hours.
